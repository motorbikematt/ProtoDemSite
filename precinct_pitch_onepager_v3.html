Problem 1 — Structural opacity

Most Democratic voters don't know their precinct captain exists.

The party's most local layer — the elected precinct representative — is functionally invisible. Captains are often appointed rather than elected, uncontested, and unreachable. The machinery meant to represent voters represents itself instead.

Problem 2 — Captain bottleneck

Captains are expected to do broadcast work without broadcast tools.

Writing a neighborhood update means manually tracking council agendas, parsing school board minutes, and composing content from scratch — entirely on volunteer time. Most captains simply don't. The communication layer that should exist between the party and its constituents is functionally offline.

Problem 3 — Voter apathy

Apathy is a rational response to a broken interface, not an indifference problem.

The civic cost of staying engaged — two-hour meetings, opaque agendas, fragmented platforms — exceeds what most working people can sustain. The standard fix is a turnout campaign. The actual fix is rebuilding the information channel.

The causal chain: Voters don't know the machinery exists because captains lack the tools to tell them — and captains lack the tools because no one has built them. Voter apathy is downstream of captain burnout. Solve the bottleneck; reduce the apathy.

The solution

A precinct-level civic publishing platform with address-resolved governance maps, LLM-summarized official content, and a structured constituent opinion channel — spanning local, state, and federal offices.

The map layer shows governance boundaries (precincts, wards, school districts, legislative districts) and resolves any tapped address to its full representation stack. The content layer ingests official documents at every tier and produces digests captains can edit and push to their subscribers. The opinion layer converts subscriber responses into geo-tagged sentiment data — usable by the party as precinct health intelligence and by government bodies as structured pre-meeting public input. A participation layer closes the loop: upcoming meetings, public comment windows, town halls, in-person organizing events.

Primary — precinct captain

Research done. They curate and publish.

Digests are auto-generated for their exact precinct geography. Their job becomes editing and voice — not research. Newsletter, lit drop, or event push in one step. Activist activities can be appended to any item.

Secondary — resident subscriber

Enter an address. Know what governs it.

Full civic stack resolved: precinct captain, ward, school district, council seat, state leg, congressional seat. Subscribe, respond to polls, and find out where to show up in person.

Step

01 — Ingest

Official documents — agendas, minutes, legislative records — ingested across local, state, and federal tiers and summarized by LLM into plain-language digests per jurisdiction.

Step

02 — Publish

Captain reviews, edits, and publishes their precinct digest as a newsletter, lit drop sheet, or feed push. Activist events and action items can be appended to any entry.

Step

03 — Engage

Subscribers read, respond to polls, post opinions on agenda items. Every response is geo-tagged to the subscriber's full jurisdiction stack — no self-identification required.

Step

04 — Act

A participation layer surfaces what to do next: upcoming meetings, public comment windows, town halls, and in-person organizing events — all scoped to the subscriber's address.

Revenue — primary

Party & club subscriptions

County parties, Democratic clubs, and state party chapters pay a SaaS subscription for a captain-facing publishing dashboard and a party-facing engagement layer — participation rates, precinct health scores, and digest delivery analytics. SDDC as founding customer.

Revenue — secondary

Small government sentiment

City councils, school boards, and township trustees pay for structured voice-of-constituent input tied to specific agenda items — replacing public comment theater with anonymous, geo-tagged sentiment collected before and after each meeting.
